# Glossary - Legal Terms Explained

### Quick Reference Guide

Your plain-language guide to legal planning terms used throughout Kanu.

---

### Legal Documents

**Will**

A legal document that says what should happen to your assets and who should care for your children after you pass away. Without a will, the court decides these things for you.

**EPOA (Enduring Power of Attorney)**

A legal document that lets someone you trust make decisions for you if you become unable to make them yourself. There are two types: property (money and assets) and personal care (health and living arrangements).

**Executor**

The person you choose in your will to carry out your wishes, manage your estate, and distribute your assets after you pass away.

**Attorney**

In the context of EPOA, this is the person you appoint to make decisions on your behalf. They are not a lawyer - just someone you trust.

**Guardian**

The person you name in your will to care for your children if something happens to you.

---

### Māori Land Terms

**Whenua**

Land. In Māori worldview, whenua has deep spiritual and cultural significance beyond just property ownership.

**Whānau**

Family or extended family group. In legal planning, this often includes your wider network of relatives and close connections, not just immediate family.

**Tamariki**

Children.

**Mokopuna**

Grandchildren or descendants.

**Tūpuna**

Ancestors or grandparents.

**Tikanga**

Customary practices, values, and protocols that guide how things should be done in Māori culture.

**Kaitiaki / Kaitiakitanga**

Guardian or guardianship. The concept of caring for and protecting resources (like land) for future generations, not just owning them.

**Taonga tuku iho**

Treasures or precious things passed down from ancestors. Can include land, objects, knowledge, or cultural practices.

**Hui**

A gathering or meeting, often used for important family discussions and decisions.

---

### Māori Land Ownership

**Māori Land**

Land that has a special legal status under Te Ture Whenua Māori Act (Māori Land Act). It has different rules from general land to help keep it in Māori ownership.

**Māori Land Court**

A special court that handles matters related to Māori land, including succession, ownership changes, and governance.

**Succession**

The process of passing on ownership of Māori land from someone who has died to their whānau. This must go through the Māori Land Court.

**Nominated Successors**

People you officially name while you're alive to inherit your Māori land interests. You file this with the Māori Land Court.

**Multiple Ownership**

When many people (sometimes hundreds) share ownership interests in the same block of Māori land. Each person owns a share, not a specific piece.

**Land Incorporation**

A formal structure that manages Māori land on behalf of multiple owners, similar to a company.

**Land Trust**

Another structure for managing Māori land collectively, where trustees make decisions for the benefit of beneficiaries.

**Ownership Schedule**

An official document from the Māori Land Court showing all the current owners of a particular block of Māori land and their share sizes.

---

### Legal Planning Concepts

**Estate**

Everything you own when you pass away - property, money, belongings, land interests, and other assets.

**Assets**

Things you own that have value, such as houses, cars, bank accounts, investments, businesses, or land interests.

**Beneficiary**

A person who receives something from your will or trust.

**Legal Assurance**

Ongoing monitoring and education to keep your legal plans current as your life changes, rather than just creating documents once and forgetting them.

**Intestate**

When someone dies without a valid will. The law then decides how their assets are distributed.

**Testator**

The person who makes a will (you, when you're creating your will).

---

### Court & Legal Processes

**Probate**

The legal process of confirming that a will is valid and giving the executor authority to carry out the deceased person's wishes.

**Family Court**

The court that handles matters like appointing guardians or property managers when someone doesn't have an EPOA.

**Property Manager**

Someone appointed by the Family Court to manage another person's finances and property if they don't have an EPOA and cannot make decisions themselves.

**Welfare Guardian**

Someone appointed by the Family Court to make personal care and health decisions for someone who cannot make these decisions themselves and doesn't have an EPOA.

---

### Planning Actions

**Review**

Looking over your legal documents to check they still match your current life circumstances and wishes.

**Update**

Changing your legal documents to reflect new circumstances, like births, deaths, new assets, or changed relationships.

**Succession Planning**

Thinking ahead about who should inherit your assets and how to make that process smooth for your whānau.

**Life Events**

Major changes in your life that should trigger a review of your legal documents, such as births, deaths, marriages, separations, buying property, starting businesses, or children turning 18.
